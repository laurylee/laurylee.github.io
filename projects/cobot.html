TMS Cobot

UBC ENPH 459 | 2023-2024

Overview

In collaborating with a team of four engineering physics students, we assisted in the development of a robotic system that autonomously conducts Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) of the brain for treatment-resistant depression using a UR cobot. The final system was capable of autonomously positioning the arm based on a stereo IR camera feed and maintaining its relative position to the patient target through a closed-loop feedback controller.

Details

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) is a non-invasive neurostimulation technique in which a magnetic field is used to induce neural activity in patients with treatment-resistant depression. Sponsored by the NINET lab from the UBC Department of Psychiatry, our capstone team assisted in developing a system to automate the procedure and increase the precision of the treatment. Since treatments can last upwards of 15 minutes, the system needed to compensate for patient movements and continually shadow the targeted area with a TMS coil while maintaining its relative position with centimeter precision. By using a stereo camera and fiducial markers to identify the patient's position and the TMS coil's position, the patient's MRI brain scan could then be digitally mapped into the same image space using a series of 3D affine transformations. From there we determined the position error and developed a PD control algorithm to control the linear and angular velocity of the cobot's end effector.
