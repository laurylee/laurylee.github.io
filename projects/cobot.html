TMS Cobot

UBC ENPH 459 | 2023-2024

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS) is a non-invasive neurostimulation technique in which a magnetic field is used to induce neural activity in patients with treatment-resistant depression. Sponsored by the NINET lab from the UBC Department of Psychiatry, our capstone team of four Engineering Physics students assisted in developing a system to automate the procedure and increase the precision of the treatment. To achieve this goal, the system needed to continually shadow the targeted area with a TMS coil while maintaining its relative position with centimeter precision.


A stereo camera identifies the position of "rigid bodies" using three IR reflective markers. We tranformed the position and orientation of the "patient" from image space (the camera coordinate system) to robot space (the cobot's base coordinate system) using a series of three-dimensional affine transformations. We determined the difference, or the error, between the current cobot pose and the desired robot pose based on where the "patient" is. A PD (proportional and derivative) control algorithm was used to control the linear and angular velocity of the TMS coil on the end of the cobot arm. We also explored using the integrated force/torque sensor to maintain a constant, light pressure on the "patient head" and to implement safety measures.
